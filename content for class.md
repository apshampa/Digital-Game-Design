# Course Title: Creating Video Games – The Duet of Game Design

## 📖 My Course Philosophy & Overview
At its core, I teach game design as an intricate, intertwined duet—a *Jugalbandhi*—between the architect of the system and the agency of the player. 

When my students enter the classroom, they often expect to immediately start building their ultimate "dream game". However, my primary focus in this course is teaching them **how to solve complex, creative problems collaboratively as a team**. I provide them with hands-on experience in project management, team dynamics, and cross-disciplinary communication, all rooted in my fundamental philosophy that **play is the voluntary overcoming of unnecessary obstacles**.

## 🏗️ Class Structure: The Iterative Journey
I have built the course around a sequence of four team-based game projects that progressively scale in size, duration, and complexity. 

*   **Project 1: Low-Fidelity Prototyping (1 Week)**
    I start my students off in small teams of three, giving them just one week to design a non-digital, paper-based prototype. I emphasize that these prototypes are highly disposable tools meant for rapid brainstorming and testing core mechanics without the friction of code.
*   **Project 2: Digital Prototype & Project Management (2 Weeks)**
    I then scale the teams up to six members to build a digital prototype. This phase is where I introduce game engines, version control, and the fundamentals of Agile software development. I require my students to use Scrum tools like product backlogs, user stories, and design change logs to manage their workflow.
*   **Project 3: User Interface & Usability (2 Weeks)**
    For their second digital prototype, I focus the teams on UI and maximizing usability through independent focus testing. I teach them to bridge the gap between their mental model as a designer and the player's mental model, ensuring they are designing games around "meaningful decisions" and trade-offs.
*   **Project 4: Humanitarian Serious Games (8 Weeks)**
    In my final capstone, teams expand to eight members for an eight-week development cycle. I have them act as a real-world studio working for an external client—such as the Red Cross/Red Crescent Climate Centre. They must design complex, serious games dealing with humanitarian topics like climate change adaptation, disaster preparedness, and disease prevention.

## 📚 Core Concepts & Academic References
To elevate my students' practical design skills, I weave rich academic and industry frameworks into the curriculum:

*   **The Anthropology and Semiotics of Play:** I teach my students to treat game design as designing a "culture in miniature". My readings reference anthropologist Clifford Geertz's *Balinese Cockfight* as a semiotic text, and I utilize Roger Caillois’ architecture of play categories: *Agon* (competition), *Alea* (chance), *Mimicry* (simulation), and *Ilinx* (vertigo).
*   **Anatomy of a Meaningful Decision:** Drawing on Sid Meier's philosophy that "a game is a series of interesting choices", I teach the core interactive loop: providing clear awareness (cues), allowing active player action, and delivering immediate consequences that ripple into long-term effects.
*   **The Aesthetic Triad:** I ask my students to evaluate games holistically through three intersecting layers that must sing the same song: *Systems* (the rules and logic), *Style* (the audio-visual polish and tone), and *Fiction* (the narrative context).
*   **Testing as Ethnography:** I train my students to observe human behavior in controlled environments, making sure they understand the distinction between *Verification/QA* ("Did we build the thing right?") and *Validation/Playtesting* ("Did we build the right thing?").

## 🛠️ The Studio Mindset: My Guiding Rules
On day one, I provide my students with a "cheat sheet" of core principles for surviving game development—and creative collaboration—in the real world:

1.  **Shoot for the moon, scale to the screen:** I tell them to brainstorm wildly, then ruthlessly reduce their scope.
2.  **Work face to face and communicate:** I remind them that great games fail because of poor communication, not just bad code. 
3.  **Test early, test often:** I constantly push them to trust the playtesting data over their own egos.
4.  **Prioritize, integrate, and cut features early:** I drill into my teams that a small, functioning game will always beat a sprawling, broken masterpiece.
5.  **Sleep is awesome:** Finally, I advise them to avoid 3D, avoid network code, *always* use version control, and most importantly, get some sleep!.
